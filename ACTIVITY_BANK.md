# Together For Health — Activity Bank

Hands-on, age-appropriate activities that teach each health topic. One flagship
activity per **topic × age group**, plus an **All-Ages** set that works for any topic.

**Age groups:** 🟢 Grades 4–6 · 🟡 Grades 7–9 · 🔵 Grades 10–12 · 🌐 All ages

**Card format (for the designer):** every activity has *Type · Group · Time*, **Materials**, **Goal**, **Steps**, and a **Debrief**. Keep that shape consistent across all cards.

## 🧠 Mental Health

### 🟢 Feelings Thermometer (Grades 4–6)
*Hands-on / SEL · whole class or small groups · 20 min*
- **Materials:** printed thermometer template (one each), crayons/markers, a feelings word list, 6 scenario cards ("lost your toy," "first day at a new school").
- **Goal:** build emotional vocabulary and learn that feelings come in different sizes — and that's normal.
- **Steps:** 1) Introduce the thermometer: calm/green at the bottom → big feelings/red at the top. 2) Read a scenario card. 3) Each student colors how high the feeling would go and picks a feeling word. 4) Share a few — show the same event can feel different to different people. 5) Practice one "cool-down" breath to bring the thermometer down.
- **Debrief:** "All feelings are okay. Big feelings get smaller when we use a calm-down tool or talk to someone."

### 🟡 Map Your Support (Grades 7–9)
*Reflection / drawing · individual then pairs · 25 min*
- **Materials:** blank paper, colored pens, a sample support-map on the board, a printed resource list (Kids Help Phone 1-800-668-6868, school counsellor).
- **Goal:** identify personal coping tools and a support network *before* a hard day.
- **Steps:** 1) Center circle = "Me." 2) Branch 1 = people I trust; Branch 2 = activities that reset me; Branch 3 = professional/anonymous resources. 3) Fill it in. 4) Add one resource everyone didn't already know. 5) Pair-share one branch.
- **Debrief:** "Knowing your map ahead of time means you don't have to figure it out in the moment."

### 🔵 Mental Health: Myths vs. Facts (Grades 10–12)
*Discussion / critical thinking · small groups + whole class · 30–40 min*
- **Materials:** 12–15 statement cards (mix of myths and facts about mental illness), two labeled areas (MYTH / FACT), a sourced fact sheet for the reveal.
- **Goal:** dismantle stigma with evidence and practice challenging misinformation.
- **Steps:** 1) Groups sort all cards into MYTH/FACT. 2) Reveal each with the science. 3) For every myth, the group writes a one-line "what I'd say if I heard this." 4) Share responses aloud.
- **Debrief:** "Stigma is the #1 reason people don't get help. Knowing the facts makes you someone a friend can come to."

## 💙 Depression

### 🟢 The Cloudy Day (Grades 4–6)
*Story + craft · whole class · 25 min*
- **Materials:** a short illustrated story (or felt board) of a character with a "cloud" that won't go away, paper "sunshine" cutouts, markers.
- **Goal:** tell the difference between a passing sad day and sadness that sticks — and know helpers exist.
- **Steps:** 1) Read the story. 2) Ask: "Is this a one-day cloud or a stuck cloud?" 3) Brainstorm who could help (parent, teacher, doctor) and what brings sunshine. 4) Each child writes/draws a helper on a sunshine cutout for a class "Sunshine Wall."
- **Debrief:** "If a cloudy feeling won't go away, telling a trusted grown-up is the bravest, smartest move."

### 🟡 Spot the Signs (Grades 7–9)
*Scenario sort · small groups · 25 min*
- **Materials:** 6–8 short vignette cards about a peer, a "warning signs" reference sheet, three labels: *Ordinary bad week / Worth checking on / Get an adult now*.
- **Goal:** recognize warning signs (withdrawal, sleep/appetite changes, hopeless talk) and know the next step.
- **Steps:** 1) Groups read each vignette and place it under a label. 2) Justify their choice. 3) Reveal the guidance. 4) For "worth checking on," draft one caring opener.
- **Debrief:** "You don't diagnose — you notice, you ask, and you connect them to help."

### 🔵 Checking In on a Friend (Grades 10–12)
*Role-play / skill-building · pairs · 35 min*
- **Materials:** conversation scaffold cards ("I've noticed… I'm here… can I help you talk to someone?"), a "what not to say" list, local resource handout, 9-8-8 crisis line info.
- **Goal:** build confidence to start a hard conversation and route a friend to real help.
- **Steps:** 1) Facilitator models a poor vs. a good check-in. 2) Pairs practice both roles. 3) Debrief how each role felt. 4) Everyone completes a wallet-size resource card.
- **Debrief:** "Listening without trying to fix it, plus connecting them to a professional, is exactly enough."

## 🫁 Vaping

### 🟢 Looking After Your Lungs (Grades 4–6)
*Demo + game · whole class · 20 min*
- **Materials:** 2 clear jars, cotton balls, picture cards (fresh air, exercise, plants / smoke, vapor), a pinwheel or candle prop.
- **Goal:** lungs are precious, some things harm them, and it's okay to say "no thanks."
- **Steps:** 1) Sort picture cards into "good for lungs / not good." 2) Breathing game: "smell the flower (in), blow the candle (out)." 3) Practice a friendly, confident "No thanks!" 4) Show clean cotton-ball "lungs" stay white with clean air.
- **Debrief:** "Our lungs help us run, sing, and play — we take care of them."

### 🟡 How Vape Ads Hook Teens (Grades 7–9)
*Media analysis · small groups · 30 min*
- **Materials:** printed examples of real vape ads/flavors/packaging, highlighters, a "tactics" checklist (kid-friendly flavors, bright colors, influencers, "safer" claims).
- **Goal:** see vaping as a product engineered to hook teens — and build resistance.
- **Steps:** 1) Groups annotate ads, circling every persuasion tactic. 2) Share findings. 3) Rewrite one ad to tell the truth about the product. 4) Post the "honest ads."
- **Debrief:** "When you can see the manipulation, it loses its power over you."

### 🔵 The Real Cost of Vaping (Grades 10–12)
*Stations / mixed · groups rotate · 40 min*
- **Materials:** 4 station sheets — (a) What's in the aerosol (ingredient cards), (b) Cost calculator + calculators, (c) Nicotine & the teen brain, (d) Refusal-skills role-play prompts.
- **Goal:** integrate the health, financial, and social angles and rehearse real refusals.
- **Steps:** 1) Groups spend ~8 min per station, completing a takeaway at each. 2) Finish on the refusal station, practicing responses to realistic pressure. 3) Each person writes one line they'll actually use.
- **Debrief:** "Knowing the facts *and* having your words ready means you're prepared before the moment ever comes."

## 🥗 Nutrition

### 🟢 Build a Balanced Plate (Grades 4–6)
*Hands-on sort · pairs · 20 min*
- **Materials:** paper-plate templates, laminated food cards or play food, a plate guide (½ fruit/veg, ¼ protein, ¼ grain).
- **Goal:** see what a balanced plate looks like and learn to "eat the rainbow."
- **Steps:** 1) Sort food cards by color and food group. 2) Build a balanced plate. 3) Check it against the guide. 4) Each names one new colorful food to try this week.
- **Debrief:** "More colors on your plate means more ways food helps you grow."

### 🟡 Sugar Shock (Grades 7–9)
*Demo / guessing game · whole class · 25 min*
- **Materials:** empty containers (pop, juice, energy drink, "vitamin" water), sugar cubes, teaspoons, label cards.
- **Goal:** spot hidden sugar, read labels, and rethink everyday drinks.
- **Steps:** 1) Line up the drinks. 2) Teams guess teaspoons of sugar in each. 3) Reveal by stacking the real number of sugar cubes. 4) Read the labels; find a lower-sugar swap.
- **Debrief:** "Liquid sugar adds up fast — water and milk are the everyday winners."

### 🔵 Reading Past the Label (Grades 10–12)
*Analysis · small groups · 35 min*
- **Materials:** real packages/labels (incl. a "natural/low-fat" product and a plain one), serving-size worksheet, a claims glossary.
- **Goal:** read nutrition panels critically and separate marketing from nutrition.
- **Steps:** 1) Calculate the *real* per-serving sugar/sodium (watch serving sizes). 2) Compare front-of-box claims to the actual panel. 3) Rank the products. 4) "Design an honest label" for the worst offender.
- **Debrief:** "The front of the box is an ad. The truth is always on the back."

## 🏃 Physical Activity

### 🟢 Animal Moves (Grades 4–6)
*Movement game · whole class · 15 min*
- **Materials:** animal cards (bear, frog, flamingo, cheetah), open space, music (optional).
- **Goal:** movement is fun, gets your heart pumping, and comes in many forms.
- **Steps:** 1) Draw an animal card; everyone moves like it for 30s. 2) Alternate fast (cheetah) and slow/balance (flamingo). 3) After a fast round, place a hand on your chest to feel your heartbeat.
- **Debrief:** "Moving your body makes your heart strong and your mood happy."

### 🟡 Heart-Rate Lab (Grades 7–9)
*Experiment · pairs · 25 min*
- **Materials:** stopwatch/phones, a heart-rate chart handout, open space.
- **Goal:** understand how activity affects the heart and introduce target zones.
- **Steps:** 1) Find your pulse; count resting heart rate (15s ×4). 2) Do 60s of jumping jacks. 3) Recount immediately. 4) Plot resting vs. active; time how fast it recovers.
- **Debrief:** "A stronger heart recovers faster — that's fitness you can actually measure."

### 🔵 Design a Workout Week (Grades 10–12)
*Planning · individual · 30 min*
- **Materials:** a weekly planner template, exercise "menu" cards, a guidelines sheet (150 min/week + strength 2×).
- **Goal:** apply real guidelines to build a sustainable personal plan.
- **Steps:** 1) Audit your current week honestly. 2) Slot in "movement snacks" you'd actually do. 3) Balance cardio + strength + flexibility. 4) Set one measurable goal for two weeks.
- **Debrief:** "Consistency beats intensity — short blocks you'll actually keep doing win every time."

## 😴 Sleep

### 🟢 Build a Bedtime Routine (Grades 4–6)
*Sorting · whole class · 20 min*
- **Materials:** routine picture cards (bath, brush teeth, screens off, story, dim lights), a pocket chart or magnets.
- **Goal:** learn a calming wind-down order and that screens come off early.
- **Steps:** 1) Scramble the cards. 2) Students order them into a good routine. 3) Find the "screen" card and place it *off* early. 4) Act out the routine together with yawns.
- **Debrief:** "A calm routine is how we tell our brain it's time to sleep."

### 🟡 Sleep: Fact or Fiction (Grades 7–9)
*Game · teams · 25 min*
- **Materials:** play money, statement cards (e.g., "you can catch up on weekends"), a fact sheet.
- **Goal:** correct common sleep myths (catch-up sleep, screens, caffeine).
- **Steps:** 1) Read each statement. 2) Teams "bid" on the ones they think are TRUE. 3) Reveal myth/fact after each. 4) Tally winnings; crown the savviest team.
- **Debrief:** "Your phone and weekend sleep-ins are quietly wrecking your school week."

### 🔵 Fix Your Sleep Schedule (Grades 10–12)
*Calculation + planning · individual · 30 min*
- **Materials:** a sleep-log worksheet, calculator, a blue-light/caffeine fact card, a sample teen schedule.
- **Goal:** quantify sleep debt and apply sleep hygiene to real constraints.
- **Steps:** 1) Estimate last week's sleep each night. 2) Compute total debt vs. the 8–10h target. 3) Identify three fixable culprits. 4) Rebuild a realistic evening routine with a fixed wake time.
- **Debrief:** "Same wake time every day + screens off early are the two biggest wins."

## 🌊 Stress

### 🟢 The Calm-Down Jar (Grades 4–6)
*Craft + practice · individual · 25 min*
- **Materials:** clear jars/bottles, water, glitter or glitter glue, strong glue for the lids.
- **Goal:** a concrete calm-down tool and practice with slow breathing.
- **Steps:** 1) Make the jars. 2) Shake = a busy, upset mind. 3) Breathe slowly (belly like a balloon) while the glitter settles. 4) "As the glitter settles, your mind settles too."
- **Debrief:** "When feelings get big, breathe slow and let your mind settle like the glitter."

### 🟡 What You Can Control (Grades 7–9)
*Sort + technique · whole class · 25 min*
- **Materials:** sticky notes, a two-column poster (Can control / Can't control), a box-breathing visual.
- **Goal:** focus energy on what's controllable and learn a portable calming tool.
- **Steps:** 1) Write current worries on stickies. 2) Sort into the two columns. 3) For one "can control," name a tiny next action. 4) Practice 4-4-4-4 box breathing together.
- **Debrief:** "Spend your energy on your circle of control — and breathe through the rest."

### 🔵 Build a Stress Toolkit (Grades 10–12)
*Stations · groups rotate · 35 min*
- **Materials:** station cards (box breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, 5-4-3-2-1 grounding, cognitive reframing, 2-min movement), a timer, a personal "toolkit" template.
- **Goal:** experience several evidence-based techniques and commit to a personal plan.
- **Steps:** 1) ~5 min per station, actually doing each technique. 2) Rate each 1–5 for "would I use this." 3) Assemble your top three into an exam-season wallet card.
- **Debrief:** "Pre-loading your toolkit means you reach for it automatically when pressure hits."

## 🛡️ Bullying

### 🟢 Crumpled Heart (Grades 4–6)
*Demo · whole class · 20 min*
- **Materials:** paper hearts (one each), markers.
- **Goal:** understand that unkind words leave lasting marks, and practice kindness.
- **Steps:** 1) Each student says an unkind phrase aloud and crumples their heart. 2) Try to smooth it flat again. 3) Notice it never fully recovers. 4) Brainstorm kind words and decorate a fresh heart for a "Kindness Wall."
- **Debrief:** "Saying sorry helps — but kind words from the start keep hearts smooth."

### 🟡 Be an Upstander (Grades 7–9)
*Role-play · small groups · 30 min*
- **Materials:** scenario cards (hallway, group chat, lunchroom), an "upstander strategies" handout (distract, support the target, report).
- **Goal:** practice concrete, *safe* ways to intervene.
- **Steps:** 1) Read a scenario. 2) Act the bystander version. 3) Replay it as an upstander using one strategy. 4) Discuss which moves felt realistic and doable.
- **Debrief:** "You don't have to be a hero — small moves shift the whole room."

### 🔵 Handling Cyberbullying (Grades 10–12)
*Analysis + decisions · small groups · 35 min*
- **Materials:** a realistic online-scenario handout, an excerpt of the school code of conduct, a reporting-options sheet, info on when it becomes harassment.
- **Goal:** navigate digital harm and know your rights and reporting paths.
- **Steps:** 1) Read the case and map who's involved. 2) Decide the response, including documenting (screenshots) and *not* engaging. 3) Choose the reporting channel. 4) Identify where it crosses into criminal harassment.
- **Debrief:** "Save evidence, don't engage, report through the right channel — and support the target."

## 🫀 CPR & First Aid

### 🟢 Calling 911 (Grades 4–6)
*Role-play · pairs · 20 min*
- **Materials:** toy phones, a simple "my address" card, scenario pictures (emergency vs. not).
- **Goal:** kids can recognize an emergency, call 911, and give key information calmly.
- **Steps:** 1) Sort pictures: emergency or not? 2) Practice "911 — what's your emergency?" and stating name + location. 3) Practice taking a calm breath and getting a grown-up.
- **Debrief:** "You can be a helper: call, say where you are, and get a trusted grown-up."

### 🟡 Hands-Only CPR (Grades 7–9)
*Hands-on skill · rotate on manikins · 30 min*
- **Materials:** CPR manikins or firm cushions, a way to play 100–120 bpm songs ("Stayin' Alive"), a step poster.
- **Goal:** confident hands-only CPR and knowing when to use it.
- **Steps:** 1) Check responsiveness ("Are you okay?"). 2) Call 911 / point to someone to call. 3) Hand placement on the chest center. 4) Compress to the beat for 60s; rotate so everyone goes.
- **Debrief:** "Push hard, push fast, don't stop — you can keep someone alive until help arrives."

### 🔵 First Aid Scenarios (Grades 10–12)
*Stations / hands-on · groups rotate · 40 min*
- **Materials:** manikins, an AED trainer unit, triangular bandages, scenario cards, floor space for recovery position.
- **Goal:** integrated, hands-on emergency response across common situations.
- **Steps:** 1) Rotate ~8 min per station: CPR + AED, choking, severe bleeding, recovery position. 2) Perform each skill. 3) A peer checks you against a skill checklist.
- **Debrief:** "In a real emergency, trained hands act. Get certified — a few hours can save a life."

## 💪 Body Image

### 🟢 What Your Body Can Do (Grades 4–6)
*Craft + reflection · individual · 25 min*
- **Materials:** large paper (or a body-outline worksheet), markers.
- **Goal:** appreciate the body for what it *does*, not how it looks.
- **Steps:** 1) Trace or decorate a body outline. 2) Label parts with what they let you do ("legs let me run," "hands let me draw"). 3) Share one amazing thing your body did today.
- **Debrief:** "Bodies are for doing amazing things — and every body is a good body."

### 🟡 Spot the Edit (Grades 7–9)
*Media literacy · whole class · 25 min*
- **Materials:** before/after edited photo examples, screenshots of editing apps/filters, discussion prompts.
- **Goal:** recognize that online images are curated and unrealistic.
- **Steps:** 1) Guess which images are edited. 2) Reveal the edits. 3) Discuss how endless scrolling fuels comparison. 4) Each lists 3 accounts that make them feel good vs. bad.
- **Debrief:** "Most of what you scroll is edited — comparing yourself to a filter isn't fair to you."

### 🔵 Diet Culture & Your Feed (Grades 10–12)
*Critical analysis · small groups · 35 min*
- **Materials:** stat cards (diet-industry revenue, how feeds rank "ideal" bodies), example posts, a reflection sheet.
- **Goal:** analyze how platforms and the diet industry profit from insecurity, and build a personal counter-script.
- **Steps:** 1) Examine how algorithms push an "ideal." 2) Look at the diet industry's financial incentive. 3) Draft body-neutral self-talk. 4) Make a concrete feed clean-up plan.
- **Debrief:** "Health isn't a size. Curating your feed *and* your self-talk is real self-care."

## 🌐 All-Ages Activities (work for any topic — scale the language up or down)

### Check-In Circle
*Discussion · circle · 10 min*
- **Materials:** none (optional talking object).
- **Goal:** normalize sharing and read the room's mood.
- **Steps:** 1) Each person shares a Rose (high), Thorn (low), and Bud (something they're looking forward to). 2) Younger: "happy / hard / hopeful." 3) No fixing — just listening.
- **Debrief:** "Naming how we feel is the first health skill — and you're not the only one feeling it."

### Health: Myth or Fact
*Game · teams · 20 min*
- **Materials:** topic question slides/cards (scale difficulty by age), buzzers or hands, scoreboard.
- **Goal:** surface misconceptions on any topic in a fun, low-pressure way.
- **Steps:** 1) Read a statement. 2) Teams call MYTH or FACT. 3) Reveal with a one-line explanation. 4) Keep score; quick prize.
- **Debrief:** "A lot of 'common knowledge' about health is wrong — now you've got the facts."

### Gratitude Wall
*Creative · ongoing/booth · 5 min per person*
- **Materials:** butcher paper or sticky notes, markers, a wall or board.
- **Goal:** boost mood and belonging; works for mental health, stress, bullying, body image.
- **Steps:** 1) Prompt ("one thing you're grateful for" / "a kind note to a stranger"). 2) Everyone adds one. 3) Leave it up for the day/event.
- **Debrief:** "Small positives, made visible, shift the whole space."

### Affirmation Cards
*Make-and-take · booth · 5 min per person*
- **Materials:** pre-printed affirmation cards + blank ones, pens, stickers.
- **Goal:** a take-home reminder; pairs with any topic.
- **Steps:** 1) Pick or write an affirmation. 2) Decorate it. 3) Take it for a mirror/locker/binder.
- **Debrief:** "What you tell yourself matters — keep a good line where you'll see it."

### Two Truths & a Wellness Goal
*Icebreaker · any group · 10 min*
- **Materials:** none.
- **Goal:** warm up a group and surface real health goals.
- **Steps:** 1) Each shares two true facts about themselves + one wellness goal (disguised among them). 2) The group guesses which is the goal. 3) Quick cheer for each goal.
- **Debrief:** "Saying a goal out loud makes it real — and gives people a way to cheer you on."

### Run a Health Fair
*Stations / event · school-wide · 45–90 min*
- **Materials:** one table per topic, each with a 5-minute activity from this bank, a resource card, and a sign-in/passport sheet.
- **Goal:** expose a whole school to every topic in one engaging loop.
- **Steps:** 1) Set up one station per topic. 2) Visitors get a "passport" stamped at each. 3) Completed passports enter a prize draw. 4) Each station ends with a takeaway/resource.
- **Debrief:** "A little hands-on time at every topic beats one long lecture."

### Exit Tickets
*Reflection · individual · 5 min*
- **Materials:** small slips or a sticky note per person.
- **Goal:** lock in learning and gather feedback after any session.
- **Steps:** 1) Each writes "one thing I learned" + "one thing I'll try." 2) Drop in a bin on the way out. 3) The club reads them to improve next time.
- **Debrief:** "Naming one takeaway makes you far more likely to actually use it."
